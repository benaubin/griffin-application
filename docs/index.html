I made this.

It didn't feel right to turn in a sheet of paper to a school that may define the rest of my life. I wanted to create something that not only answers the questions you ask of applicants but also allows me the creative freedom to share my nature and interests in a more meaningful way.

This is what I came up with.

I am a developer.

I use code to solve problems. At the start of 6th grade, I was trying to find a homework planner that worked for me, but as hard as I looked, I couldn't find one. I decided to create my own. I taught myself how to code, and over the course of a year, I created PlanHW. It worked great. I shared it with friends, many of whom loved using it, and it solved my problem. I still use code to make amazing websites, apps and games.

I am a story-teller.

Telling stories is amazing. I started a student publication with some friends to explore topics that matter to our city. For example, I wrote an article about a controversial development plan off of Shoal Creek, a topic that dominated city politics. I love the creative experience of telling stories in many ways with whatever tools I can use, whether it's video, graphics, or interactive experiences.

I am a leader.

I work with groups that get amazing things done. I led a team of middle-schoolers as we planned our annual Global Festival, a winter celebration of all cultures. In addition, I am part of the No Place for Hate committee at my school, a group of students who create activities that promote respect and diversity throughout the year. Last Thursday, I worked with a group of other students to reignite a school fundraiser that was left in chaos after being abandoned by a previous group.

I am a student.

I've been at ACE for the past 3 years. Part of going there meant I could take challenging courses years above my grade level. The most rewarding experiences in school are when I finally turn in that paper I worked so hard on or when I am able to do things that once felt almost unimaginable before after learning something new.
